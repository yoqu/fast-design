# Causes of Color — WebExhibits

A large, well-regarded educational exhibit on **why things are colored** — the physical and
chemical *mechanisms* that produce color, organized around Kurt Nassau's "fifteen causes of
color." This is the canonical free reference for the physics/chemistry side of color (as opposed
to perception or design).

## The Framework — Nassau's 15 Causes of Color

Nassau classifies every color phenomenon by its physical mechanism. The exhibit groups these as
**Made light / Lost light / Moved light**. The mechanisms (by category):

**Vibrations & simple excitations**
1. **Incandescence** — hot bodies glowing (filament, lava, blackbody).
2. **Gas excitations** — emission lines (neon signs, sodium lamps, aurora, fireworks).
3. **Vibrations & rotations** — molecular modes (why water/ice are pale blue).

**Transitions in ligand fields** (crystal/ligand field theory)
4. **Transition-metal compounds** — e.g. turquoise, malachite, chrome-oxide green.
5. **Transition-metal impurities** — same ion in a host lattice: Cr³⁺ gives ruby (in corundum)
   *and* emerald (in beryl) — host changes the color.

**Transitions between molecular orbitals**
6. **Organic compounds** — conjugated double bonds / chromophores (most dyes, biological
   pigments — chlorophyll, carotenoids, anthocyanins).
7. **Charge transfer** — electron hops between ions (sapphire's blue = Fe→Ti charge transfer;
   Prussian blue).

**Transitions involving energy bands** (solid-state / band theory)
8. **Metals** — band structure sets reflectivity (gold yellow, copper red, silver neutral).
9. **Pure semiconductors** — band gap sets the cutoff (vermilion/HgS, cadmium yellows, diamond).
10. **Doped / impurity semiconductors** — dopants add levels (blue diamond from boron; LEDs).
11. **Color centers** — lattice defects trapping electrons (amethyst, smoky quartz, some
    fluorite); irradiation-induced color.

**Geometrical & physical optics** (no absorption needed — color from how light is bent/split)
12. **Dispersive refraction** — prisms, rainbows, the "fire" of diamond.
13. **Scattering** — Rayleigh/Tyndall: blue sky, blue eyes, red sunsets, blue smoke.
14. **Interference** — thin films and multilayers: soap bubbles, oil slicks, many structural
    colors. (See also [iridescent color math](iridescence-thin-film-interference.md).)
15. **Diffraction** — gratings & periodic structure: opal, some beetles/butterflies, CDs.

The exhibit also covers **eye & brain**, **colorblindness**, **animal vision**, and **color
theory** sections beyond the 15 physical causes.

## Chapter 7 — Pigments (the section the user landed on)

Chapter 7 covers biological/material pigments. Sub-sections include: Pigments, Flowers, Animals,
Humans, **Fall & fruit**, Flamingos, Cabbage, Chlorophyll, and a Do-It-Yourself.

**Page 7H — Plant pigments in fruit & flowers**
(<https://www.webexhibits.org/causesofcolor/7H.html>). The four major plant-pigment families:

| Family          | Color range            | Examples / notes                                                          |
| --------------- | ---------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Chlorophylls** | Green                  | Drive photosynthesis. Structurally similar to **heme** (porphyrin ring).  |
| **Carotenoids**  | Yellow / orange / red  | Absorb blue–indigo. Mango, carrot, fall leaves; **lycopene** → tomato/watermelon red; **astaxanthin** → salmon & cooked-shellfish red. |
| **Flavonoids**   | Pale yellow → red/blue/purple | Latin *flavus* = yellow. Lemons, berries, chocolate, wine. Includes **anthocyanins** (pH-sensitive reds/purples/blues) and proanthocyanidins. |
| **Betalains**    | Red-violet & yellow-orange | Only in the **Caryophyllales** (beets, bougainvillea, cacti). Mutually exclusive with anthocyanins in a plant — never co-occur. |

Mechanistically these are mostly **cause #6 (molecular-orbital / conjugated-bond) colors** — long
conjugated chains (carotenoids) and chromophore ring systems (chlorophyll, anthocyanins) absorb
specific visible wavelengths. The page also notes **bilirubin** — long known as the animal
hemoglobin-breakdown pigment — is also produced in plants when chlorophyll is broken down.

## Why It Matters (for the skill)

- **The "why is it colored?" reference.** When a question is about a *material's* color cause
  (gemstone, pigment, sky, metal, butterfly), this taxonomy is the right lens — and it cleanly
  separates **absorption/emission causes (1–11)** from **structural/optical causes (12–15)**.
  Structural color (interference/diffraction) has no pigment and shifts with viewing angle; that
  distinction matters for both science answers and CG/material rendering.
- **Same ion ≠ same color.** Cause #5 (ruby vs. emerald from the same Cr³⁺) is a great corrective
  to "this element makes this color" thinking — the host lattice / ligand field sets the result.
- **Pairs with perception refs.** This covers the *stimulus* side; pair with the perception/metric
  references ([Koenderink](koenderink-3d-metric-field-rgb.md),
  [Bujack non-Riemannian geometry](bujack-non-riemannian-color-geometry.md),
  [how eyes turn light into color](how-eyes-turn-light-into-color.md)) for the full chain from
  physics → eye → perceived color.
